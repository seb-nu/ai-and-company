# The Pyramid Principle — Detailed Reference

_Barbara Minto's Pyramid Principle is the foundational communication framework at MBB firms._

---

## The Structure

```
              [Governing Thought]
             /         |         \
      [Arg 1]       [Arg 2]       [Arg 3]
      /    \        /    \        /    \
   [E1]  [E2]   [E3]  [E4]   [E5]  [E6]
```

- **Level 1 (Top)**: The single governing thought — your recommendation or key finding
- **Level 2**: The 3–5 arguments that together prove Level 1
- **Level 3**: The data, facts, and evidence that prove each Level 2 argument

## Writing the Governing Thought

The governing thought must:
- Be a complete sentence (not a topic label)
- Express the full answer (not just a direction)
- Be supportable by exactly the arguments below it

**Weak**: "Our cost structure"
**Strong**: "Our cost structure is uncompetitive and requires $40M in reductions by Q3 to restore margin"

## Inductive vs. Deductive Grouping

**Inductive grouping** (most common in consulting): Arguments are different kinds of the same thing.
Example: Three reasons why we should enter Market X.
- Test: Can you summarize the group with a single statement?

**Deductive grouping**: Each argument leads logically to the next (A → B → C → therefore D).
Example: Situation → Complication → Resolution.
- Use sparingly; harder to follow for executives scanning quickly.

## The SCQA Framework (Extended SCR)

For communication to skeptical or senior audiences, use SCQA:

- **S**ituation: Accepted context (no new information)
- **C**omplication: What changes or challenges the situation
- **Q**uestion: The question the complication naturally raises (often implied)
- **A**nswer: Your governing thought — the answer to that question

Example:
- S: "We operate in 12 markets and have delivered 15% CAGR over 5 years."
- C: "Three of our top markets are showing margin compression as local competitors gain share."
- Q: "How do we protect profitability while maintaining growth?"
- A: "We should exit two subscale markets and reinvest the freed capital in our top three."

## Executive Summary Slide

The executive summary is the pyramid on one slide:
- Title = Governing thought (the full recommendation)
- Body = 3 bullets = Level 2 arguments (one sentence each)
- Sub-bullets (optional) = key supporting facts

The executive summary should be written **last** but presented **first**.
It is a synthesis, not an introduction.

## Common Pyramid Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| Governing thought is vague ("We have opportunities") | Make it specific ("We have three untapped opportunities worth $120M") |
| Arguments overlap (not MECE) | Restructure so each argument covers a distinct dimension |
| Arguments don't prove the governing thought | Check: if all arguments are true, does the governing thought logically follow? |
| Too many levels (4+) | Flatten; most audiences can only absorb 3 levels |
| Arguments are topics, not insights | Rewrite each as a complete sentence with a "so what" |
